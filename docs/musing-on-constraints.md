# Constraint as Craft

There is a seductive fantasy surrounding AI-generated work. It says that today’s roughness is temporary, tomorrow’s models will be brilliant, and eventually the gap between generation and judgment will disappear. In that future, the machine will not just assist the maker; it will become the maker. It will one-shot finished applications, clean architectures, coherent designs, and reliable products so effortlessly that all this talk of correction, guardrails, and process will seem quaint. It is a comforting story, especially for people who want technological progress to function like magic. But it rests on an assumption that deserves much more suspicion than it usually gets: the assumption that getting better at producing convincing output is the same thing as getting better at producing trustworthy output.

That assumption is not obviously true. In fact, experience suggests the opposite. Systems often get dramatically better at seeming right before they get meaningfully better at being right. They become more fluent, more polished, more persuasive, more legible, more complete-looking. Their failures stop announcing themselves. The danger shifts from obvious wrongness to plausible wrongness. And plausible wrongness is the most dangerous kind, because it invites people to stop checking. The problem is no longer that the machine is embarrassingly broken. The problem is that it is believable enough to be trusted without having earned that trust.

I know this pattern because I have already lived through it in another medium. I was an art director for a decade, and when image generation first arrived it was total garbage. Its failures were loud, easy to mock, almost cartoonish. Anatomy was broken. Text was smeared. Background logic dissolved on inspection. Hands looked cursed. Perspective wandered. Composition often felt like a dream someone forgot how to finish. It was easy, at that stage, for professionals to dismiss the entire category. But that dismissal missed something important. Even in its worst state, the technology had strengths. If you understood those strengths and refused to be fooled by its weaknesses, you could make it useful. Not perfect. Useful.

That usefulness did not come from trusting the model. It came from constraining it. It came from rejecting the parts it was bad at, exploiting the parts it was unusually good at, and then using real human skill to close the gap between generated output and finished work. The job was never “let the image generator cook.” The job was to art direct the machine. To inspect relentlessly. To count fingers. To read the background text instead of assuming it was fine. To notice the places where coherence was only cosmetic. To bring judgment to bear where the machine supplied fluency but not reliability. That is what made the work real. Not belief in the machine, but disciplined correction of the machine.

What fascinates me now is that software engineering has wandered into the same territory, but many people do not yet recognize it. The argument around AI-written code often collapses into two caricatures. On one side are the evangelists, who treat every sign of model competence as evidence that software engineering itself is about to disappear. On the other side are the skeptics, who look at generated code, laugh at the slop, and conclude that the whole thing is unserious. Both positions miss the more interesting truth. AI can already generate a surprising amount of useful code. It is also already capable of generating an astonishing amount of dangerous, plausible, brittle nonsense. These two facts are not contradictory. They are the actual reality.

I build products with AI-written code. I do not write code myself. That fact will strike some developers as ridiculous, even offensive. They will look at the outputs and say, correctly, that much of it is sloppy, overextended, under-architected, inconsistent, or superficially competent in ways that hide deeper problems. I do not reject that criticism. I take it seriously. In fact, my whole project emerges from taking it seriously. The response is not to insist that the code is secretly fine. The response is to ask: how do we close the gap?

That question is the center of my philosophy. Not “is AI good enough yet?” Not “will future models save us?” But: how do we take what AI is already good at and surround it with deterministic systems that force it toward what we actually want? How do we make correction part of the substrate rather than an occasional act of heroism? How do we turn judgment into environment?

The answer I keep arriving at is constraint. Not as punishment. Not as fear. As craft.

One of the most formative realizations for me came from something as simple as using Rust after spending time around more permissive environments. In JavaScript, a lot of bad code can “seem to work.” The system gives you a long leash. You can wander quite far into structurally dubious territory before anything stops you. That permissiveness can feel productive in the moment, but it also lets weak solutions harden into accepted ones. Rust felt different. Rust kept saying no. No, this ownership story is not clean enough. No, this abstraction is not honest enough. No, this apparent shortcut is not actually safe enough. That denial was frustrating at first, but it also had a clarifying effect. The compiler forced convergence. It denied me the pleasure of a sloppy solution that merely seemed to work. And in doing so, it often pushed me toward something cleaner, more principled, and more durable than I would have reached otherwise.

That experience changed how I think about AI. If denial and constraint can improve human engineering outcomes, why would they not improve machine-generated ones? If permissive environments let weak ideas survive, then of course a generative system will exploit that softness. It will generate code that satisfies the nearest visible requirement and move on. That is what such systems do. They minimize friction. If you want something better, you do not ask them politely to have more taste. You create an environment where the wrong paths are missing.

This is why I keep returning to the same core insight: AI is perfectly capable of being corrected, but only if correction is real. Not advisory. Not a style guide buried in docs. Not a prompt saying “please write clean code.” Real correction means that bad paths are denied, weak habits are interrupted, ambiguous output is forced through stronger filters, and the machine cannot easily do what you do not want it to do. In other words, best practices have to become executable.

That is what my suite of tools is really about. Ishoo is not just an issue tracker; it is a way of making project structure, execution flow, and acceptance criteria explicit enough that an agent can be forced through them. Neti is not just a linter; it is an enforcement layer for code quality, architecture, and assumptions that would otherwise remain hand-wavy. SEMMAP is not just documentation; it is a way of making architecture inspectable and navigable so the machine cannot pretend a codebase is simpler than it is. omni-ast is the semantic primitive layer that lets deeper reasoning happen over actual structure rather than vibes. Testy is an attempt to make test adequacy something more rigorous than “I guess this feels covered.” Each of these tools closes a gap. Together, they begin to form a system in which software engineering judgment is no longer trapped inside the head of a senior engineer who shows up late to criticize the output. It becomes part of the environment itself.

This is where the work becomes philosophical rather than merely practical. I do not believe the future lies in AI replacing engineering judgment. I believe the future lies in engineering judgment being externalized into systems that AI is forced to work through. That is a very different vision. It does not imagine the human disappearing. It imagines the human’s standards becoming operational. It imagines a world where process, architecture, style, safety, testing, and issue flow are not just remembered, but encoded. Not as inflexible bureaucracy, but as the deterministic skeleton that generative systems need in order to produce reliable work.

And this is why I am not especially frightened by the common objection that “in five years the models will be so good they will just one-shot entire applications perfectly.” Maybe they will become astonishing. Maybe they will one-shot much more than they can today. But how do you know when “astonishing” becomes “trustworthy”? How do you know when “looks coherent” becomes “is coherent”? How do you know when architecture is actually sound rather than merely fluent? How do you know whether the tests prove anything real? How do you know whether an abstraction is robust, or simply stylistically plausible? These are not rhetorical questions. They are the real questions. And the answer cannot just be, “the model felt smarter this year.”

My tools can answer those questions more honestly than hype can. They are designed to tell you, in deterministic terms, whether the output withstands scrutiny. They are meant to be the systems that keep mattering even as model quality improves, because better generation does not remove the need for verification. It intensifies it. The more persuasive the output becomes, the more valuable it is to have machines that can say, with confidence, “close, but not good enough.” The need for criticism does not vanish when the machine gets smoother. It becomes more important because the errors are no longer embarrassing enough to catch themselves.

This brings the analogy back to image generation. Image models are incomparably better now than they were when they first appeared. They can produce images that would have seemed impossible a few years ago. And yet it is still dangerous to accept an image straight out of the model without real scrutiny. You still have to ask whether the text is legible, whether the anatomy actually holds, whether the implied logic of the scene survives inspection, whether the image is communicating what it only seems to communicate at first glance. The professional eye still matters. Not because the tool failed to improve, but because improvement changed the failure mode from obvious wrongness to seductive wrongness.

I think code will follow that same arc. We will get better and better generators. We will get outputs that look increasingly professional. We will get architectures that feel more complete, tests that feel more comprehensive, abstractions that feel more elegant. But unless those outputs are met by deterministic systems of inspection, constraint, and guided correction, we will simply have produced more sophisticated slop. Not ugly slop. Convincing slop. And convincing slop is exactly the kind that ships.

So I do not see my work as fighting against model progress. I see it as building the layer that makes model progress usable. If the models get better, my apps do not become obsolete. They become more necessary and more powerful. They get fed stronger material. They get to enforce higher standards. They become the answer to a harder question: not “can the AI generate something,” but “can the AI generate something that survives serious judgment?” That is a much more durable place to stand.

In the end, I am not trying to prove that AI is already perfect, and I am not trying to defend every line it writes. I am trying to build the world that lets AI converge. The lesson I learned from image generation was not that the machine was secretly an artist. The lesson was that a messy, incomplete, powerful generative system can be made reliable when a human with standards refuses to accept its weaknesses and instead builds a process around them. I am doing the same thing now in software. The medium changed. The thesis did not.

Constraint is not the enemy of AI creativity. Constraint is what turns AI output into something worth trusting. The future will not belong to whoever believes in the models the hardest. It will belong to whoever knows how to shape the environment they operate in.
